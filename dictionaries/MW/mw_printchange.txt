
This file documents changes made to the digitization of MW in which the
digitization is intentionally different than the printed edition.
The reasons for these changes are various.
Most are deemed 'scan' errors; only a few are thought to be errors
of interpretation by Monier-Williams.
Sanskrit words are usually spelled with SLP1 transliteration.
NOTE (Nov 13, 2015):  This file was formerly kept as file
  'corrections-factual.txt' in the https://github.com/sanskrit-lexicon/MWS/
  It was begun approximately in 2008.

37. April 8, 2010.
    headword 'iti' (L=28747) changed to have accent.
    Also, the occurences of 'iti' in the body of 28747 also had accent added.
2010-03-22: James Fitzgerald
Hi, I've just noticed that in both the online MW and the image of the dictionary the udatta accent on the first syllable of iti is missing. As a look at the Petersburg dictionary will confirm, the first syllalbe of iti should have, in roman script, an acute mark over it. This was MW's error, and he failed to correct it in his corrigenda. I imagine it's not possible to correct the scanned image, but the online dictionary should be made correct.

2010-03-29: Peter Scharf
 I think it is proper to make factual corrections to the dictionary if and when we can track the correction as a correction for users.  The tracked correction would indicate the editor who made the correction, when, the type of correction (factual, etc. as Jim Funderburk, Malcolm, and I discussed some time ago), and reference to an authority.  I know Jim Funderburk tracks this information but we don't yet have a means to display it.  In this case Jim Fitz. cites as authority Bohtlingk and Roth.  I haven't had a chance to check the occurence in accented texts myself.  Have you Jim Fitzgerald?

2010-03-29: James Fitzgerald
Yes, I'm looking at RV 10.71.11d right now:
the sa?hita text "yajñásya m?tra? ví mimita u tva?" [ALTERNATE TRANSCRIPTION] // yajJa(udatta)sya mA(udatta)trAM vi(udatta) mimIta u tvaH // 
 
is glossed in the padapatha at its very end with  "| ví | mimIte | u? íti | tva? |"  // vi(udatta) | mimIte | uM i(udatta)ti | tvaH | //.
 
I was led to check the dictionary because the Satapatha Brahma?a passage I was reading at the time was inconsistent and had a typo or two regarding the accent of íti. It had several other correct instances. E.g. of correct passage: SB 11.4.3.6 "sárasvati tvá??a rup??i sámanaktu yajñáir íti"
 
[ALTERNATE TRANSCRIPTION]  //sa(udatta)rasvatI tva(udatta)STA rUpA(udatta)Ni sa(udatta)manaktu yajJa(udatta)ir i(udatta)ti //

Dfzwa  Supplement record L=334490 changes the accent.  Sharon asks whether it should also be changed in compounds.  Peter removed the accent from the compounds since it is just inherited in the digital dictionary while in print, judging from the compounds with vowel initial words where sandhi is done, accent is not inherited. 98. Apr 14, 2014
'tavy/a' changed to 'tavy^a' for several (75)
records.  See updlogs/log_20140413-01.txt for list.
This requested by Peter Scharf, who provides the
following comment:
The grave accent mark is the typical way of Romanizing the svarita accent 
in Sanskrit.  The reason is was chosen is because it graphically represents a 
falling tone, which is how the ancient Indian phonetic treatises describe the 
svarita.  It is NOT the way the Sanskrit Library has chosen.  
The SL has chosen the circumflex/carot character instead because this is the 
symbol generally used for the accent in European languages.  Furthermore the 
logic of the choice of the grave accent mark for svarita is not cogent.  
The acute accent is used by Sanskrit scholars for the transcription of the 
udAtta accent, which is not a rising tone but a high tone.  The svarita is 
represented by a vertical stroke, not a sloping stroke, in the Indian 
traditions where the svarita is the highest tone, even where it is not 
described as a falling tone but as a tone higher than udAtta.  The vertical 
stroke is also used for the udAtta in traditions where the udAtta is the 
highest tone.  In any case, the grave accent mark in MW represents a svarita, 
not an anudAtta.  The Cologne data-entry merged grave and acute accent marks 
in the acute mark throughout.  Only two marks are necessary to mark three 
accents in the European transcription; the acute accent mark marked the udAtta,
 the grave accent mark marked an independent svarita, the  anudAtta and 
dependent svarita were left unmarked.
